Artifical Intelligence.

Introduction

Artificial intelligence can be defined as a subset of computer science that is focused on building computer systems that have human level intelligence so that machines can perform tasks that require human level intelligence. These tasks may range from everyday things such as communicating with humans as chatbots or reading texts to more specialised procedures such as coming up with mathematical proofs or designing and building software.

There are a multitude of different types of AI, but we can classify them in two main categories:

  • General AI – General AI has human level general intelligence and the ability to learn and would act in a way that is indistinguishable from normal humans. Unlike specialised AI, general AI can solve any problem and multiple different tasks at the same time. For example, it would be able to hold a conversation while also being able to process what its cameras are seeing.
  • Specialised AI - Specialised AI is built to carry out one specific task, but to do it very well and can only perform under a narrow range of parameters and context. This means that when this type of AI faces a problem that it wasn’t specifically designed to solve, it is unable to solve it and fails. Some common examples include self-driving cars, Siri, email spam filters and facial recognition software.

History

The idea of AI started in the 1950s with Alan Turing. Turing reasoned that humans use available information as well as reason in order to solve problems and make decisions, so he questioned the ability of machines to do something similar. He proposed a “seed AI” (kind of like a stupid baby AI) that would be able to learn from its experiences and “grow up” into a general AI. However, he was unable to get started on AI because computers were extremely expensive to run and buy (IBM 650 that was developed mid-1950s cost the equivalent of $4 million today).

Since the 1950s, there have been multiple waves of AI funding and “AI winters” where funding receded. The first wave of funding came in 1956 when the first AI program, Logic Theorist, was presented at the Dartmouth Summer Research Project on Artificial Intelligence. This program was designed to perform automated reasoning and eventually proved 38 of the first 52 theorems in Principia Mathematica while also finding new and more elegant proofs for some.

From 1957 to 1974, AI flourished since computers had been rapidly improving every year due to Moore’s Law (the exponential growth of transistors on a microchip) which meant that you could run more computationally intensive machine learning algorithms and already existing ones would run in shorter time. However, once the research into AI had started they realised that they had reached a massive obstacle. Even though computers were becoming more powerful they were still too slow and lacked sufficient memory to do anything substantial. This led to the first “AI winter”.

In the 1980s, AI was reignited by the popularization of expert systems. The theory was that the program would ask an expert on how they would respond to a given situation and store their answer. The program would do this for every situation and eventually be able to give advice to a non-expert on how to respond. This was big jump in the 1980s and Japan invested 400 million dollars into revolutionising computer processing, implementing logic programming, and improving expert systems. Sadly, they failed to meet their goals and another AI winter began.

In the 1990s, AI started to flourish again since the internet brought with it large amounts of data to train machine learning algorithms on as well as hardware improvements. The largest breakthrough for deep learning (a subset of machine learning) was using GPU’s highly parallel processing power to analyse and scan through large datasets quickly which shortened the time to train deep learning models significantly. Currently, we are in the middle of high investment in AI due to the large breakthroughs in the field of machine learning in the last decade. We now have specialised AI that can perform tasks better than or as good as a human would be able to do. Some of these tasks are handwriting recognition, natural speech recognition and playing games such as Chess or Go.

Future

Going forward we should expect more exciting breakthroughs, especially since general AI hasn’t really taken off yet so there is a lot of potential there. The exciting part comes after we finally construct a human level general intelligence. Why? Well a human level AI is not limited by biological conditions. Unlike biological beings, machines can evolve rapidly by simply improving their hardware and their source code which means a human level AI can recursively improve its own cognitive ability by improving its code and by becoming smarter the AI can improve its code even more. Another thing that machines aren't limited by is necessities. Biological beings need water, food, shelter, mates etc whereas an AI would need none of those things, it would operate on a much longer time scale than humans and would only be concerned about ensuring that it has enough resources to run its program. Last but not least, an AI is not limited by the speed of its neurons firing. Electricity travels at the speed of light which is much faster than our nerve impulses. This means that the AI can do many more computations than we can in a given second which allows it to progress a lot quicker than humans. After improving itself to a certain degree, the human level general intelligence will become a superintelligence.

The pros of a superintelligence are immense. One pro may be that a superintelligence would be able to colonize space, for example by using Von Neumann probes (probes which can self-replicate in space) to mine other planets or asteroids. Another advantage is that humans may also live on Mars if the superintelligence succeeds in terraforming Mars. Furthermore, by improving our medical knowledge, techniques and drugs humans will be able to increase the average life expectancy and maybe even be able to upload their consciousness into VR. These are just a few of the many benefits superintelligence could bring.

On the other hand, if the superintelligence is handled incorrectly it may lead to an existential catastrophe. A few examples would be if a person with misguided motives obtained the superintelligence they could effectively take over the world or if someone assigns the superintelligence a task and while completing the task the superintelligence decides eliminating humans would help it complete its task. There is no way to predict what it would do since its cognitive ability would be beyond our comprehension. This leads to the control problem; how can we get the AI to do what we want it to do? Two possible solutions are:

  • Offload the control problem to the superintelligence - Rather than specifying what we want the superintelligence to do we should instead tell the superintelligence to do what we would want it to do. Of course, it isn’t as simple as that as the superintelligence may misinterpret what we would like it to do. In Eliezer Yudkowsky’s paper Coherent Extrapolated Volition (CEV), he tells us that CEV means people's choices and the actions people would collectively take if "we knew more, thought faster, were more the people we wished we were, and had grown up closer together." This means that the superintelligence would do what majority of people would want it to do even if they don’t know it at the time. This stops it from misinterpreting since it isn’t what one person would want but rather what many different types of people would want.
  • Stunt the superintelligence’s potential - There are multiple ways to do this, for example the superintelligence may only reply YES/NO to questions we ask or may be in a location where it is completely blocked off to the outside world. The risk here is that since we are facing a superintelligence it is very likely that it would eventually find a way to break out (for example emotionally manipulating the operator into letting him out) and this means we need a longer-term solution

To use superintelligence correctly, solving the control problem is vital.

Closing remarks

We are in a very exciting age for AI where we have breakthroughs in specialised AI every year while we edge closer and closer to general AI. Eventually, by using a superintelligence humans’ prosperity will boom as the superintelligence solves each problem, such as climate change, poverty and starvation, that troubles us. Of course, the viability of superintelligence application in our daily lives comes down to the control problem and whether a solution is found in time or not.
